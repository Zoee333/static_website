Art as a Hobby: My Journey from Traditional to Digital

The Beginning: Traditional Art

Art has always been a passion of mine—a way to express creativity, unwind, and bring ideas to life. What started as simple sketches on paper has evolved over the years into a deeper exploration of digital art. Like many artists, my journey began with pencil and paper. I spent countless hours sketching, shading, and experimenting with different techniques. Traditional art taught me patience, precision, and the importance of fundamentals like perspective, anatomy, and lighting. There’s something irreplaceable about the tactile feel of graphite on paper or the blend of watercolors.

The Transition to Digital Art

A few years ago, I decided to explore digital art. At first, the transition was challenging—getting used to a graphics tablet, learning new software, and adapting to a screen-based workflow took time. But the possibilities were endless:

  • Undo button (a lifesaver!)
  • Layers for non-destructive editing
  • Endless brushes and textures
  • Easy color adjustments

Programs like Procreate, IbisPaintX, and Clip Studio Paint became my new canvas. While I still love traditional art, digital tools opened up a world of efficiency and experimentation.

My Digital Process

One of the most rewarding aspects of digital art is seeing a piece come together in stages. Below is a breakdown of my process for one of my recent works:

1. Sketching

I start with rough, loose lines to define the composition. Digital sketching allows for quick adjustments—no need to erase endlessly!

2. Line Art

Once the sketch feels right, I refine the lines, often using a clean ink brush. Some artists skip this step for a more painterly style, but I enjoy crisp outlines.

3. Base Colours and Flatting

Next, I block in flat colors on separate layers. This step is like a coloring book—simple but crucial for establishing the palette.

4. Shading and Lighting

Here’s where the piece starts to feel alive. I use layer modes like Multiply for shadows and Overlay for highlights to add depth.

5. Details & Finishing Touches

Finally, I add textures, adjust colors, and polish small details. Sometimes, I revisit older steps to tweak proportions or lighting.

Final

Art is a journey, not a destination. Whether you prefer pencils, paints, or pixels, the most important thing is to enjoy the process. If you’re thinking of trying digital art, don’t be intimidated—every artist starts somewhere, and mistakes are just part of the learning curve.
